Overview

I am interested in solving combinatorial optimisation problems using Answer Set Programming in a computational biology context. Such logic programming paradigm can apply to modelling the metabolism of organisms. Metabolic networks are obtained by inferring the presence of metabolic reactions based on the genomic content of the associated organism. Such networks are the basis of models that can be simulated using various mathematical frameworks among which flux balance analysis (FBA) or network expansion. The latter is notably perfectly suited to logic programming and is implemented in many software systems that I developped (see below). As an example, the Menetools package includes several tools enabling dedicated to exploring the producibility potential of metabolic networks.

Optimisation problems are numerous in the field of metabolic modelling. A first one that I worked on is the refinement of metabolic network or gap-filling. It aims at selecting a limited number of metabolic reactions from databases in order to ensure a metabolic objective is met by the metabolic model. This is the purpose of the Python package Meneco (PLOS Computational Biology 2017). In addition to Meneco, I created, in collaboration with scientists of Potsdam University, a hybrid method for metabolic gap-filling, enabling the reconciliation of flux balance analysis and network expansion modellings. This work is implemented in Fluto (LPNMR Conference 2017, best Student Paper, extended version of the manuscript in Theory and Practice of Logic Programming 2019).

I dedicated the past few years to applying optimisation problems and metabolic modelling to communities of organisms. MiSCoTo is my first project on this topic. The underlying optimisation problem (Bioinformatics 2018) aims at selecting minimal communities within a collection of metabolic networks such that the selected communities meet a metabolic objective characterised by the producibility of metabolic compounds. MiSCoTo can in addition suggest metabolic exchanges that would be needed between the species of the selected communities.

One of my latest projects if the development of Metage2Metabo or M2M (eLife 2021, in production), an all-in-one workflow permitting the global analysis of a possibly large community of organisms in the context of microbiota. M2M is a flexible pipeline with the following main steps: automation of metabolic network reconstruction with Pathway Tools, computation of individual and collective metabolic capabilities, estimation of the added-value of cooperation within the community, selection of minimal communities and identification of key species with respect to a metabolic objective.

  abstract = {Metabolic networks play a crucial role in biology since they capture all chemical reactions in an organism. While there are networks of high quality for many model organisms, networks for less studied organisms are often of poor quality and suffer from incompleteness. To this end, we introduced in previous work an answer set programming (ASP)-based approach to metabolic network completion. Although this qualitative approach allows for restoring moderately degraded networks, it fails to restore highly degraded ones.           Abstract
          Systems biology relies on computational biology to integrate knowledge and data, for a better understanding of organisms’ physiology. Challenges reside in the applicability of methods and tools to non-model organisms, for which data is limited, and more generally to host-microbiota systems. Understanding the interactions in the later is an objective of systems ecology. Metabolic networks are a useful solution to model them functionally. In this direction, several semantics exist and are at the core of metabolic network reconstruction, particularly for their refinement through gap-filling. Gap-filling is a combinatorial problem that aims at selecting reactions in databases to ensure the feasibility of a behaviour by the model. It is a very crucial step due to various pitfalls: model overfitting, false positive, choice of functionality semantics. This thesis aimed at better understand these limits and propose solutions to them. As a first results part, we benchmarked several gap-filling algorithms to assess the value of graph-based semantics with respect to the constraint based one. Then we propose a hybrid gap-filling method that reconciles both semantics. Finally, we extended the gap-filling problem towards the selection of communities and the screening of metabolic functions within large microbiotas. Problems modelled and solved during this PhD were applied to brown algae metabolism and to the human gut microbiota.
